I came across an interview where Mark ranked Blink-182’s albums, and the early ones — Cheshire Cat, Dude Ranch, even Buddha — were sitting at the bottom.

And honestly, I get it.

They’re rough, inconsistent, and straight up immature. You can hear a band that’s still figuring itself out.

But those are also the records that stuck with me the most.

I didn’t start with that version of Blink.

Like most people my age, I got into Blink-182 through Enema of the State and Take Off Your Pants and Jacket — the more polished, “pop punk” version of the band. By the time I was actually paying attention to music, they had already gone through their peak and were already broken up.

I was born in 1996, so my early exposure to music came at a weird time. MTV was still around, but at least in Latin America it was already leaning heavily into reality shows. Music was still there, but it was whatever was popular at the time — emo, pop, reggaeton. Bands like Blink-182 weren’t really part of that moment anymore, at least not in the way they used to be.

So for me, Blink wasn’t this raw punk band. It was this bigger thing I discovered later — a band that had already become mainstream, but also one that didn’t take itself seriously at all. They were immature, saying the most unhinged stuff in interviews, running around naked, and at the same time writing some of the catchiest songs I’d ever heard.

And that ended up being the gateway.

Getting into that version of Blink naturally pushed me backwards. The more I listened, the more I got into punk as a whole — and eventually into the kind of sound that Blink originally came from. When I finally landed on their earlier stuff, it didn’t feel like going backwards.

It felt like finding the root of something I already liked. That feeling starts with Cheshire Cat.

## Cheshire Cat (1995)

Cheshire Cat is probably the easiest example of why Mark ranks those albums so low.

There’s no real cohesion, the production is rough, and some songs just don’t land. But at the same time, you get moments that don’t really exist in later Blink.

“Carousel” alone has one of the greatest bass lines they ever wrote. “M+M’s” shows they already had a sense for melody and catchy riffs. And something like “Touchdown Boy” is just raw energy — fast, messy, but impressive when you think about Tom playing and singing it live.

Then you get songs like “Wasting Time,” which feel very teenage in a different way — more daydreaming than punchline. Or “Does My Breath Smell?” leaning into that self-deprecating humor that became part of their identity.

It’s not a great album.

But it feels real in a way their later records don’t try to be.

## Buddha (1998 re-release)

A bit of context — the original Buddha actually came out before Cheshire Cat. What most people know today is the 1998 re-release by Kung-Fu Records, which is basically a more polished version of that early demo.

So this isn’t really about evolution or a shift in sound. It’s more like a parallel version of early Blink — the same ideas, just in a slightly different form.

And honestly, part of the appeal for me is just the collector side of it.

You get alternate versions of songs that ended up on Cheshire Cat. “Carousel,” for example, has a longer intro here, and I actually prefer this version because of it. It feels a bit more drawn out, more atmospheric before everything kicks in.

But most of the album works like that — different takes on what would later become Cheshire Cat. So it ends up being a bit of a “pick your poison” situation, and if I’m being honest, Cheshire Cat usually has the better versions.

Still, there are some standout moments. Their cover of “The Girl Next Door” by Screeching Weasel is a great one — and for me it was one of those cases where a Blink song led me to discover another band entirely.

And then there’s “21 Days,” which I still think is one of their best ballads, even if it’s not something most people talk about.

At the end of the day, Buddha is more of the same as Cheshire Cat. It’s rough and it’s easy to skip.

But if you’re already into that early era, it’s one of those releases that adds a bit more depth — and a few weird, very Blink moments that you don’t really get anywhere else.

## Dude Ranch (1997)

This is where Blink-182 really starts to look like the band most people recognize.

By this point, they weren’t just playing small local shows anymore — they were already an established skate-punk band. The budget is better, the production is tighter, and you can feel that things are starting to click.

For me, this might be my favorite Blink-182 record.

This is the stage where they start writing proper songs — not just fun or chaotic ideas, but tracks that actually stick. “Dammit” is the obvious example. It’s one of their best songs, easily — simple, catchy, and it perfectly captures that awkwardness of growing up and dealing with relationships for the first time. It feels honest in a way that still defines Blink at their core.

Then you’ve got “Josie,” which leans into that idea of the “perfect girlfriend,” that kind of teenage ideal. And at the same time, they still keep their humor and references intact — like throwing in an ode to Princess Leia in *A New Hope*, which somehow fits perfectly with everything else they were doing.

This era also feels iconic in a different way. Tom was playing a modded Les Paul, Mark on a StingRay — it’s a very specific snapshot of the band before everything changed, both in sound and in scale.

What makes *Dude Ranch* stand out to me is that balance. It still has the energy and attitude of their earlier work, but now it’s structured, intentional, and way more consistent. There aren’t many weak moments here.

It feels like the midpoint between an underground band grinding it out and what they would eventually become.

And at the same time, it feels like the beginning of the end for that early version of Blink-182.

## Enema of the State (1999)

This is where that shift fully lands.

For a lot of people, this is the best Blink-182 album. And honestly, for me it’s right there with *Dude Ranch*.

The difference is that everything here feels elevated. The production — with Jerry Finn — is on another level, and it basically set the template for what pop punk would become for years after.

This album doesn’t really need an introduction. The singles alone say enough — “What’s My Age Again?”, “All the Small Things,” “Adam’s Song.” It’s hit after hit, but without losing what made them work in the first place.

At the same time, this is a new version of the band. Scott is gone, Travis Barker comes in, and everything tightens up. Travis brings a level of precision and energy that pushes them forward. It’s fair to wonder how big they would’ve become without him.

This is peak commercial Blink-182. MTV, *American Pie*, *The Simpsons* — they were everywhere. It felt like they went from a band you discover to something unavoidable.

And that’s kind of the turning point.

Songs like “Mutt” with a great bass line, and “Going Away to College” is probably one of my favorite Blink songs — it captures that coming-of-age feeling in a more grounded, less chaotic way than their earlier stuff.

It’s a great album. Probably their most complete one.

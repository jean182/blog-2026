For the past year, I’ve been collecting **4K Blu-rays**—partly for the itch of owning my own stuff, and partly to watch movies in the highest quality possible. On a nice QD-OLED, even older films can look surprisingly good.

I didn’t set out to watch a “trilogy” or anything like that. I picked these movies for completely different reasons.

*Psycho* was an easy buy since I had already seen it before and liked it. *Peeping Tom* came from curiosity—I kept seeing the name pop up when looking into early slasher influences, and the idea of a killer using a camera sounded too interesting to ignore. *Eyes Without a Face* was even more random; I found out the title inspired the Billy Idol <a href="https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=9OFpfTd0EIs" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">song</a> and just wanted to see what the movie actually was.

At some point, I realized all three came out in the same year: **1960**.

* **Psycho**, directed by Alfred Hitchcock  
* **Peeping Tom**, directed by Michael Powell  
* **Eyes Without a Face** (*Les Yeux Sans Visage*), directed by Georges Franju  

That made me go back and rewatch them more carefully. Not because I was trying to do a deep analysis, but because my original write-up felt too surface-level and I wanted to give them more credit.

What stood out is that none of these movies rely on monsters in the traditional sense. There are no supernatural forces here—the horror comes entirely from people. That’s probably why they still hold up, even if some parts feel dated.

If I’m being honest, I wouldn’t recommend these movies to most people. They can feel slow, the acting can be theatrical, and the violence is nowhere near modern standards. But if you’re into horror, or just curious about where a lot of these ideas come from, they’re absolutely worth watching.

> **Warning:** This post contains spoilers for all three movies. If you haven’t seen them, I recommend watching them first before reading on.

## Psycho: The Cultural Juggernaut

<Image
  src="/posts/when-horror-became-human/psycho.png"
  alt="Norman Bates dressed as his mother, caught mid-reveal holding a knife in the basement"
  width={3840}
  height={2160}
/>

I’ll start with **Psycho**, probably the most well-known of the three and the entry point for most people. It’s also the most accessible: black and white, driven by a legendary score, and grounded in something that feels disturbingly real. There’s nothing supernatural here—just Norman’s psyche and his obsession with his mother, which somehow makes it more uncomfortable.

What stood out to me most on rewatch is how the film shifts its perspective halfway through. You spend the first act following Marion as if she’s the protagonist, only for her to be killed off suddenly. Even if the shower scene doesn’t hit with the same shock it once did, that structural shift still works. Losing your anchor forces the story into a completely different direction.

What makes that first half so effective is how it pulls you into Marion’s decision and makes you experience its consequences with her. The film doesn’t just show her stealing the money—it makes you complicit in it. The camera lingers as she packs, the music builds tension, and you can feel the hesitation in her actions. This time around, her motivation felt much clearer to me. She isn’t acting randomly; she’s trying to escape a stagnant life and build something with Sam despite his financial situation. That context gives weight to everything that follows.

Once she’s on the road, the movie leans into paranoia. What I found interesting is that this tension doesn’t come from extreme situations, but from completely normal interactions. Her boss, the police officer, and even the car salesman all contribute to a growing sense of exposure. None of them explicitly accuse her, but the way they look at her and question her is enough to make her feel like she’s about to be discovered. The music amplifies this feeling, but it’s really her own mindset—imagining worst-case scenarios—that drives it. You’re not just watching her paranoia, you’re sharing it.

<Image

  alt="The iconic Bates Motel sign"
  width={3840}

/>

Then Norman appears, and the dynamic shifts again. After all the pressure from the outside world, he feels harmless, almost comforting. Marion lowers her guard around him in a way she never does with the others. Even when something feels slightly off, it doesn’t register as danger. That contrast is what makes the motel sequence work so well. The setting—the isolated motel, the house looming above it, and the birds both in paintings and as taxidermy—adds a subtle layer of unease without fully breaking that sense of safety.

The shower scene felt like the peak of that control for me. It’s not graphic on its own, but the rapid cuts, the music, and the framing make it feel much more violent than what’s actually shown. It’s one of those moments where the film doesn’t need to show everything—you end up filling in the gaps yourself. What stood out right after is how quickly the focus shifts. The money, which felt so important up to that point, suddenly becomes almost irrelevant. The camera even lingers on it briefly, as if to remind you, but then it disappears along with everything else. It felt less like a traditional payoff and more like a kind of misdirection, where the thing driving the entire first half ends up not mattering as much as it initially seemed.

After Marion’s death, the movie changes. It becomes more of an investigation, following new characters trying to piece together what happened. This part is still well executed, especially in Norman’s performance, but it felt less engaging to me on rewatch, mainly because I already knew the outcome. The tension in the first half comes from uncertainty and from being pulled into Marion’s paranoia. Once that’s gone, and knowing where things are heading, the second half feels more about confirming what you already suspect rather than discovering it.

## Peeping Tom: The Tragic POV

<Image
  src="/posts/when-horror-became-human/peeping-tom.png"
  alt="Mark aiming his film camera directly forward, merging the act of filming with the perspective of the viewer"
  width={3840}
  height={2160}
/>

The second movie is *Peeping Tom*, which is actually my favorite of the three. While *Psycho* was a massive hit, this movie effectively destroyed Michael Powell’s career at the time because it was considered too "filthy." From a modern perspective, though, they don’t feel that different.

What stood out to me immediately is how the movie uses perspective. The opening scene already puts you in Mark’s point of view, following his first victim through the camera. You’re not just watching him—you’re seeing what he sees. That pattern repeats throughout the film, whether he’s killing or simply spying on others. It creates this uncomfortable feeling where the act of watching stops feeling neutral.

<Image
  src="/posts/when-horror-became-human/camera-pov.png"
  alt="Mark’s first victim seen through his camera, her expression captured at the moment of fear"
  width={3840}
  height={2160}
/>

Mark himself is a strange character. He’s quiet, awkward, and almost harmless in the way he behaves. At work, taking photos or operating cameras on set, he does everything without much emotion—just enough to get by. There’s no obvious aggression in him, which makes everything else feel more unsettling. He doesn’t come across as a typical “killer,” but as someone completely consumed by a very specific obsession.

That obsession isn’t just violence—it’s recording fear. The murders are part of a “documentary” he’s making, where the goal is to capture the exact moment someone becomes aware of their own fear. The camera isn’t just a tool; it’s part of the act itself. He even uses a mirror so his victims can see their own faces, forcing them to confront that fear directly while he records it.

The movie also gives context to where this comes from. Mark’s father used to film him as a child, especially during moments of distress, turning fear into something to be observed and studied. That idea carries into adulthood, but twisted into something much darker. Instead of understanding fear, Mark is trying to capture and preserve it.

There are moments where the film almost feels lighter, especially in the scenes on the movie set. They come across as small glimpses into filmmaking itself—actors missing cues, scenes falling apart, budget issues. They’re funny, but they also reinforce how different Mark is. While everyone else treats filmmaking as work or entertainment, for him it’s something far more personal.

At the same time, he tries to connect with Helen, who sees something good in him. Those moments feel almost normal, but they never fully go anywhere. His obsession with filming—and the need to complete his project—always pulls him back. That tension between wanting connection and being unable to escape his own behavior makes him feel less like a monster and more like someone completely broken.

By the end, that obsession turns inward. As the police close in, Mark becomes part of his own film. He finally experiences the same fear he was trying to capture in others, recording himself in the process. It’s less a dramatic resolution and more a completion of what he had been building all along.

## Eyes Without a Face: The Surgical Nightmare

<Image
  src="/posts/when-horror-became-human/eyes-without-a-face.png"
  alt="Christiane wearing her expressionless white mask, lying quietly while staring ahead, evoking a fragile and trapped presence"
  width={3840}
  height={2160}
/>

*Eyes Without a Face* is a very controlled kind of horror. It doesn’t rely on chaos or shock, but on something colder and more deliberate.

What stood out to me the most is how cold and methodical everything feels. The doctor isn’t portrayed as chaotic or impulsive—he’s calculated, detached, almost clinical in the way he moves and acts. That same feeling carries into how scenes are structured. We often follow actions step by step, whether it’s him returning home or the way the assistant lures and transports victims. At first, it almost felt overly explicit to me, like the film was showing too much of the process. But the more I sat with it, the more it felt intentional. Everything is presented in this procedural way that removes urgency and replaces it with something more unsettling.

Visually, the film is striking. A lot of it looks clean and even beautiful—the lighting, the compositions, even small details like the doctor’s car. That contrast makes the horror land differently. Instead of chaos or shock, it’s presented in a controlled, almost elegant way, which makes the disturbing parts feel even more uncomfortable.

Christiane is at the center of that contrast. Her eyes are expressive, but the mask removes any visible emotion. The way she moves also feels slightly unnatural, which adds to the sense that she’s not fully present as a person anymore. The mask doesn’t just hide her face—it gives her this quiet, almost sad presence. She feels less like a character driving the story and more like someone trapped inside it.

<Image
  src="/posts/when-horror-became-human/christiane-real-face.png"
  alt="Christiane after the face transplant, showing her natural face in contrast to her earlier masked appearance"
  width={3840}
  height={2160}
/>

The surgery scene is exactly what it sounds like—a medical procedure. But what makes it uncomfortable isn’t just what’s happening, it’s how it’s presented. There’s almost no music, just breathing, tools, and the sounds of the operation. It’s slow, detailed, and hard to look away from. Even if some of the effects show their age, the scene still works because of how stripped down it is.

By the end, the film shifts into something more symbolic. When Christiane frees the animals and turns against her father, it feels less like a traditional resolution and more like a release. The house, which felt like a controlled environment or even a kind of cage, gives way to the outside world. The image of the animals escaping, especially the birds, reads as her freeing herself as well. Her father’s obsession—whether driven by guilt or ego—reduced both her and the animals to tools, and that final act breaks that dynamic.

## Influence on Modern Horror

You can see the DNA of these three movies everywhere in modern cinema:

1. **Narrative Shifts:** *Psycho* proved you can kill your lead and keep the audience hooked.
2. **The Killer's Lens:** *Peeping Tom* turned the camera into a weapon and pioneered the "killer’s POV" that would eventually lead to found footage and slashers.
3. **Atmospheric Dread:** *Eyes Without a Face* leans into that quiet, disturbing atmosphere that modern "elevated horror" movies use today.

## Final Thoughts

Looking at all three together, what stuck with me the most is how impressive they are for their time. Not just the themes, but the intention behind each film—they were trying things that still hold up today. Even if I got a bit bored at times, there’s something about them that feels ahead of what they should have been able to do in 1960.

*Peeping Tom* is the one that stayed with me the most. The whole POV idea feels so obvious now, but for that time it’s kind of insane. Watching it today, it almost feels more relevant because we’re so used to cameras being everywhere.

I wouldn’t say these movies changed how I see horror—most of their ideas have already been reused a lot. But that’s kind of the point. You can see pieces of them in everything that came after.

I’m also not someone who watches a lot of modern horror. I lean way more into slashers, especially 70s and 80s stuff, so these films sit in a weird spot where they feel older, but at the same time more intentional than a lot of what came later.

If you’re really into horror, I think you’ll at least appreciate what these movies are doing. And if you enjoy older films, they can leave a strong impression. But if you’re expecting fast pacing or jump scares, this probably won’t do much for you.
